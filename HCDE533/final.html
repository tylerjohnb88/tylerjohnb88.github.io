Project Proposal

For my final project, I plan to return to the lamp project since I didn't get a chance to do that one to the best of my ability. My goal is to create a wall sconce that incorporates a lamp with a modular shade and a potted plant.

I plan to create a mold and cast the main body out of plaster. This shape should lend itself well to a two-part mold.

Source

If, for some reason, that shape proves too difficult, a simpler cylinder will be used.

Source

The top half will be an empty space that will have to be treated in a water proof paint or glue. A small pot will likely be employed for easier potting and cleaning. The measurements of the pot will be considered in the design.

The bottom half will be where the light source is housed, with wiring exiting through the back, into a mounting plate that will either be 3D-printed or made from off-the-shelf parts. There will be room on the open bottom for modular shade plates. I plan to laser-cut, 3D-print, or mill a couple options based loosely on these styles.

Verdant Sconce

For a final project, we needed to combine multiple techniques from the quarter. My plan was to combine 3D printing, mold-making, plaster casting, and laser cutting to make a wall sconce that would double as a planter. Mold making has proved to take significantly more time than I estimate and in the future, I should plan to make more than one because it hasn't gone right on a first try yet.

The first part of the project focused on creating the 3D printed mold for the silicon mold. I knew that I didn't want to do this in Rhino ever again so I turned to Solidworks. This proved to be significantly easier for the shape I had in mind. 3D printing took two full days on two printers due to the size of the silicone mold.

After molds were 3D printed, nothing else seemed to go right. I have learned a lot from these problems, but another attempt is needed to actually produce the lamp I set out to make. My goal is to document all of these problems here for reference in my next attempt.

Process

Digital Design

The shape wasn't difficult to make in Solidworks, but thinking about the future two part mold, I had concerns that I didn't know how to ebb without trying the mold. I thought that the amount of surface area with the mold and the desired zero-draft could be problematic. Halfheartedly reassuring myself that I could simply pull the two halves apart, I pressed on.

The plan is to route the power cord through this tube by adding a third piece to the two-part mold. I had 3D printed a tube that I would glue to one half of the mold. Looking back, I don't know why I thought this was a good idea. A 3D printed straw that fits firmly onto a prominence on the silicone mold could work.

The four holes in the topmost surface are for magnets to be inserted for easy lamp shade swapping.

For mounting the sconce to the wall, I planned to use a dove tail joint, with the female half of the joint being screwed to the wall. This half would be 3D printed for now.

After spending far too long trying to learn the correct way to create a mold in Solidworks, I had to shift to manually modeling the mold. This didn't take very long, but it's not as editable as I know it could be. I would like to return to this learning opportunity later.

Fabrication

3D printing the molds went smoothly, but took two days to print on two printers due to the size. While things were printing, I got to cutting some shade components. I am so looking forward to trying more shades but, unfortunately, this part of the project would be abandoned after cutting due to problems that were about to present with the mold.

I added a paper filler to each half of the mold to cut down on the volume of silicone. This helped, but I still ended up using 2.5 liters of silicone for the larger half of the mold.

Bottom half of mold

Before pouring, I had tried to get access to a vacuum chamber, but after three days I couldn't wait any longer to pour the silicone. I poured very slowly from the bottom up, hoping that I wasn't about to waste $100 of silicone. Unfortunately, this was where the problems began.

As expected, the mold mold was difficult to remove. So dificult that it had to come out in pieces and the mold became single use. In my next attempt, I think a three-part mold is needed for the larger top half.

Once I saw the first large air bubble, I stopped trying to salvage either mold and switched to searching for the brightside in learning opportunities. Near the bottom left of the picture below, you can see the large bubbles that got trapped near the ribs where the surface tension between the silicone and the mold proved too much for the air to push through. Even in a three-part mold, I expect that a vacuum chamber will be necessary.

This whole pour was a dissapointment, but I know what to fix for next time. And, I did end up with half of a mold for plaster beads from the leftover silicone. I plan to use this mold for leftover plaster to create dense scrap to be used in the bases of future projects.

Note to self: Make your funnels bigger!

Knowing that I wouldn't be able to redesign, print, and repour in time for the due date of this assignment, I transitioned to simply tring to 3D print the whole lamp so I'd have something to show and to confirm that there weren't other features of the lamp that would need changing before a second attempt. With only two days left, I chose settings that would greatly speed up the 2.5 day print to 1.1 days.

I woke up at 4am, five hours into the print, to nightmare-inducing crunching plastic coming from my office. Guessing that the cause was the nozzle getting caught on some janky tree supports, I changed the supports and altered the shape of the wire tunnel before hitting print again.

The second time, the print seemed great, making it past the most difficult geometries. But it too made a crunching noise, indicating the death of my last ditch effort to have something cool to show for a final project, this time catching on the inner lightning supports.

I love this lamp and the challenges that mold making poses and I want to see the project through, so I will be returning to conquer this lamp over the summer!
