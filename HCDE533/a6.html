06Molds & Plaster Wrens

Echoing Wren

The task for this project was to create a 3D model of a mold for creating a two-part silicon mold that could then be used to cast multiples of the desired object.

The first part of the project focused on creating the 3D printed mold for the silicon mold. Designing a mold in Rhino is fairly easy, but there is a lot of forethought that needs to go into how everything will fit together and how liquid will flow within both molds.

Tools, Materials, & Credits

  • Rhino
  • Cura
  • 3D Printer (Ender 3 s1 Pro)
  • Protopasta PLA
  • Oomoo 30 silicone Rubber
  • Plaster

  • Wren STL Source - (STL File)
  • Autodesk Instructables: 3D Printing for Two-Part Silicon Molds - (PDF)

Process

I wanted to find something that my wife and I could paint together since plaster is just a blank canvas asking for paint. We love seeing all the house finches, black-eyed juncos, and the ocassional wren while walking around campus and since they're all all about the same size, paint could be the only distinction needed to make all three from one mold.

Searching through Thingiverse, I found this model of a wren that would work.

Digital Design

Opening the .stl file in Rhino, I started by removing the wren's legs because I knew they would be near impossible to do well with a two-part mold. I also trimmed some of it's body to create a flat base for sitting on a flat surface.

Next, I created a parting line for the mold to split on. This guide (source) on 3D printing two-part silicon molds was really helpful.

After following the steps in the rest of the guide, I had a 3D printable mold for the silicone. Or so I thought.

The pour hole was the only potential complication I foresaw. I placed it on the bottom surface so that any artifacts from the pour would be hidden, but this means that I'd have to break the funnel off of my 3D printed mold in order to remove the silicone mold.

At this point, I thought my model was near printable... it was not. I ran into a lot of problems with how the division line was modeled. It ended up taking me many more hours of learning Rhino the hard way, trying all the buttons that seemed even minorly related. I ended up changing the parting line from the complex one to a flat, centerline. This worked much better for modeling, but made for more work when it came to cleaning up the cast birds.

Mold Model (Rhino)

Fabrication

I have finally learned enough about 3D printing to start a print liike this and have it work the first time. Troubleshooting is satisfying for the learning that comes from it, but it's often more satisfying to just hit print and know your settings are correct.

Designing the mold was the hardest part. Everyhing else went smoothly and it was mostly just taking notes on what to improve next time. The silicone pour went as expected, but there were steps I would have liked to take if 3D modeling hadn't already taken all my time away. Things like placing the 3D-printed mold mold into an acetone bath to smooth the print lines and using a vaccuum chamber to remove bubbles were both things I had planned for, but ran out of time. There were two small bubbles on the inside surface of my mold and obviously the print lines would be visible on any casts.

I chose to measure by weight for better accuracy and one less cup coated in silicone. For Oomoo 30, this meant 1 part A : 1.3 part B.

Pouring slowly and starting from a low point was recommended in the Oomoo instructions and did seem to help some with bubbles. Placing strips of scrap fabric beneath the mold mold made all the difference when it came time to demold. I let the mold cure for 10 hours before demolding, but the instructions recommended 6.

Overall, it wasn't a bad mold. I was a little worried about having no keys and how much extra work this would make for me with each cast, but once I trimmed the edges and cut the keys off, it was fairly easy to line the two halves up well enough for 6 casts.

I found that 70g of plaster mix was about what this mold required. I used 20g of water with 55g of plaster. I'm not sure how much it actually helped, but my thought was that by adding a higher ratio of water than the recommended 3 parts plaster : 1 part water would help the slury fill the small shapes formes by the beak and tail.

The first cast ended up with a lot of tiny bubbles at the topside of the pour. I was able to almost entirely remove these in subsequent pours by pouring a small amount of plaster in and rotating the mold to coat all sides before filling the rest more slowly. Each pour took 20 minutes to harden enough to demold.

It wasn't long before a small flock had been hatched.


Other things I learned...
1. Leveling the botom on my model was a good idea, but it ended up causing the bird's center of mass to be too far forward. Going back to fix this would have been worth it if I were casting more than a handful of small birds. I ended up changing the angle on each bird with sand paper and a sponge and, halfway through, I found that filling the mold just below full took care of this problem.

2. Even though I was warned, my keys were still too small. On top of that, I offset my positive keys in the model (negative keys on the mold) inward to create some room for them to mate once they were silicon. They should have been offset outward, further exacerbating the problem of being too small.

3. Plaster is much easier to smooth out and finish immediately after demolding, before it has time to cool.

Lastly, more as a note to my future self, making a mold of a bunch of spheres would be great for any left over plaster. Plaster has a really nice weight to it and could be used to fill cavities in a lamp base or any product where some extra weight is needed.
